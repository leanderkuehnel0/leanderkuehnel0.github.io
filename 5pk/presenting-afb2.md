
- good friday agreement is structured in three "strands"
- one for internal democratic institutions in northern ireland
- one for north-south relations, so relations between republic and nortern ireland
- and one for east-west-relations between ireland and the UK

---
northern ireland assembly

- most important internal democratic institution is the northern ireland assembly, a parliament with 108 elected members, each self-designating as nationalist or unionist

- major decisions require both sides to agree (50% of total votes and 50% of both nationalist and unionist members)

- supposed to guarantee both nationalist + unionist voices, but has had long periods of suspension when sides can't agree, since even a small group can block important decisions

- commonly argue the structure is more about managing division than overcoming it, so it doesn’t always lead to real cooperation

---

north/south ministerial council
- most important institution for north-south-relations is 
north/south ministerial council

- consists of ministers from northern ireland executive + irish govt, working together on cross-border issues

- 6 formal areas of cooperation:
    - agriculture
    - education
    - environment
    - health
    - tourism
    - transport


- not just high-level meetings — a lot of active cooperation on these areas, through working groups and committees


- tightly linked w/ ni assembly — if assembly isn’t working, council can’t function either

- seen by nationalists as a step toward eventual irish unity; critiziced by unionists for the same reason, as it provides more influence for irish govt in NI

---
british-irish council

- one of two most important east-west-institutions is british-irish council

- consists of represent. of brit + irish govts, devolved govts from NI, scotland, wales, isle of man, channel islands

- meets at summit level 2x each year

- idea of british-irish council: get everyone talking, improve relations between all parts of these islands

- not as controversial as other institutions, but some say it’s more about keeping up appearances than making big policy changes — more of a talking shop

- does give smaller places like isle of man + channel islands a voice they didn’t have before

---
british-irish intergovernmental conference

- british-irish intergovernmental conference is the other important east-west-institution
- replaces institutions from 1985 anglo-irish agreement

- co-chaired by irish foreign affairs minister + UK sec of state for NI

- gives irish govt a voice on NI matters not devolved to local govt, so matters that british govt rules over

- mainly handles sensitive topics like security, human rights, policing — all things ni assembly can’t deal with because agreement is impossible due to requirement of nationalist and unionist majorities

- again criticized by unionists, as it gives irish govt too much influence in NI

- as result of gfa, RoI removed territorial claim for NI from its constitution
